\section{Overview}

This note documents the first extension of Igami (2017) to incorporate
\textbf{innovation spillovers / agglomeration effects} in a simplified 2-period
setting. The goal is to understand whether these effects undo the innovator's
dilemma found by Igami.

\section{Model}

\subsection{State and Firm Types}

The market state at each period $t$ is $s_t = (n_o, n_b, n_n, n_{pe})$, where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $n_o$: old incumbents (have \emph{not} adopted new technology),
  \item $n_b$: ``both'' incumbents (have adopted; produce with new tech),
  \item $n_n$: new entrants (using new technology),
  \item $n_{pe}$: potential entrants.
\end{itemize}
Active firms in the product market are those with $n_o + n_b + n_n > 0$.\\

``Both'' firms are multi-product: they continue to sell old-gen (at cost
$c_o$) while also selling new-gen (at cost $c_{n,t}$). This is the source of
the \textbf{cannibalization effect}: by expanding new-gen supply, a ``both''
firm depresses the old-gen price it receives, creating a disincentive to
innovate that is absent for pure new entrants.

\subsection{Timing Within Each Period}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Product market:} Cournot competition in \emph{two
        differentiated markets} (old-generation and new-generation products)
        with linear inverse demand:
        \begin{align}
          P_o &= A - \frac{B}{M}\,Q_o - \frac{\rho B}{M}\,Q_n, \label{eq:demand_o}\\
          P_n &= A - \frac{B}{M}\,Q_n - \frac{\rho B}{M}\,Q_o, \label{eq:demand_n}
        \end{align}
        where $\rho \in [0,1)$ is the cross-market substitution parameter.
        Old firms ($n_o$) compete only in the old-gen market at cost $c_o$.
        New entrants ($n_n$) compete only in the new-gen market at cost
        $c_{n,t}$. ``Both'' firms ($n_b$) are \emph{multi-product}: they sell
        in both markets simultaneously and internalize the
        \textbf{cannibalization} effect, in that their new-gen output reduces
        demand for their own old-gen product.
  \item \textbf{Private shocks:} Each firm draws i.i.d.\ cost shocks
        $\varepsilon_{i,a} \sim \mathrm{EVT1}(0, \sigma)$ for each action $a$.
  \item \textbf{Discrete choices} (sequential order: old $\to$ both $\to$ new $\to$ pe):
        \begin{itemize}
          \item old: $\{\text{stay}, \text{innovate}, \text{exit}\}$
          \item both/new: $\{\text{stay}, \text{exit}\}$
          \item pe: $\{\text{enter}, \text{quit}\}$
        \end{itemize}
        Each type observes the \emph{realized} outcomes of all earlier movers
        before making its own decision.
  \item \textbf{Transition} to period $t+1$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Agglomeration Extension}

The new-technology marginal cost depends on the number of firms already using it:
\begin{equation}
  c_{n,t} = c_{n,0} - \gamma \cdot (n_{b,t} + n_{n,t}), \qquad \gamma \geq 0.
  \label{eq:agglom}
\end{equation}
When $\gamma = 0$ the model reduces to the Igami baseline. When $\gamma > 0$,
a larger innovative cluster lowers costs for all new-tech firms, capturing
the knowledge-spillover or agglomeration channel.

\subsection{Value Functions and CCPs}

Because firms move sequentially within each period and each type observes
the \emph{realized} outcomes of all earlier movers, each type's decision
problem is effectively a \textit{single-agent problem}: earlier-mover
uncertainty has been resolved, and later-mover choices enter only through
pre-computed CCPs. Combined with i.i.d.\ EVT1 private cost shocks, this
yields closed-form logit CCPs at each stage:
\begin{equation}
  P(a \mid \tau, \tilde{s}) = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\bar{V}_{a}(\tau,\tilde{s})/\sigma\bigr)}
                           {\sum_{a'} \exp\!\bigl(\bar{V}_{a'}(\tau,\tilde{s})/\sigma\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{s}$ is the \emph{realized intermediate state} after all
earlier movers have acted, and $\bar{V}_a$ is the systematic utility:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{V}_a(\tau, \tilde{s}) = \pi_\tau(s_0) + \text{cost}(a) + \beta \,
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[ V_{t+1}(\tau', s') \mid \tilde{s},\, a_i = a,\, \mathrm{CCP}_{\text{later}} \right].
\end{equation}
Here $\mathrm{CCP}_{\text{later}}$ denotes pre-computed CCPs of same-type
peers and all later-moving types; flow profits $\pi_\tau(s_0)$ are evaluated
at the period-opening state.

\subsection{Solution Method}

The model is solved by backward induction, following Igami (2017):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Terminal period ($t=1$):} $V_1(\tau, s_1) = \pi_\tau(s_1)$,
        where Cournot profits use the agglomeration-adjusted cost
        $c_{n,1} = c_{n,0} - \gamma(n_{b,1} + n_{n,1})$.
  \item \textbf{Period $t=0$:} For each state $s_0$, stages are solved in
        \emph{reverse move order} (PE $\to$ new $\to$ both $\to$ old),
        exploiting the sequential structure:
        \begin{itemize}
          \item \textbf{Stage 4 (PE):} Each PE firm observes the full
                realized intermediate state $\tilde{s}$ and solves a binary
                logit $\{\text{enter}, \text{quit}\}$. We compute CCPs for
                all intermediate states reachable from $s_0$.
          \item \textbf{Stage 3 (new):} Each new firm solves a binary logit
                $\{\text{stay}, \text{exit}\}$, conditioning on pre-computed
                PE CCPs to integrate over the PE-stage continuation value.
          \item \textbf{Stage 2 (both):} Each both firm solves a binary
                logit, conditioning on new + PE CCPs. Innovators locked in
                from Stage 1 ($k_{io}$ old firms that chose innovate) are
                added to $n_b$ before this stage.
          \item \textbf{Stage 1 (old):} Each old firm solves a multinomial
                logit $\{\text{stay}, \text{innovate}, \text{exit}\}$,
                conditioning on all lower-stage CCPs.
        \end{itemize}
        No fixed-point iteration is needed within the period: the sequential
        structure makes each stage a closed-form single-agent problem given
        pre-computed downstream CCPs. Marginal CCPs for later-moving types
        are recovered by integrating over the equilibrium transition
        distribution (products of independent Binomial / Multinomial draws).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Regional Extension}

\subsection{Regional Structure}

To study the interaction between industrial policy and agglomeration we
extend the state space with a \textbf{region} attribute. There are $R = 3$
regions (labelled $r \in \{1,2,3\}$); each firm is born into a region and
never leaves it, and potential entrants sit in per-region pools $n_{pe,r}$.
Product-market competition stays \textbf{global}, with all active firms
meeting in the same two-market Cournot game of
equations~\eqref{eq:demand_o}--\eqref{eq:demand_n}. Agglomeration spillovers,
in contrast, are now \textbf{local}:
\begin{equation}
  c_{n,r,t} = c_{n,0} - \gamma_r \cdot \bigl(n_{b,r,t} + n_{n,r,t}\bigr),
  \qquad r = 1,\dots,R.
  \label{eq:local_agglom}
\end{equation}
Because $c_{n,r,t}$ varies across regions, firms of type ``both'' and
``new'' face \emph{heterogeneous} marginal costs in the new-gen market. The
Cournot stage therefore loses symmetry across regions, and equilibrium
quantities are obtained by solving an asymmetric FOC system of up to $4R$
equations per state. Within-region slots remain symmetric (all firms in the
same $(type, r)$ cell pick the same quantity), so per-firm profits are still
characterised by $\pi_{o,r} = b q_{oo,r}^2$, $\pi_{n,r} = b q_{nn,r}^2$, and
$\pi_{b,r} = b(q_{bo,r}^2 + q_{bn,r}^2) + 2 s\, q_{bo,r} q_{bn,r}$ where
$b = B/M$ and $s = \rho B/M$.\\

Each firm privately captures the lower cost when it innovates but does not
internalize the benefit it confers on regional peers. This is a standard
\textbf{local agglomeration externality}, creating a wedge between the
social and private return to innovation and opening the door to regionally
targeted industrial policy.

\subsection{Solution with Regional States}

The sequential-move structure (old $\to$ both $\to$ new $\to$ pe) carries
over unchanged, and we extend the same idea one level down: \textbf{within
each stage, regions move sequentially}, $r = 1 \to 2 \to 3$. When firms in
region~$r$ decide, they observe the realized outcome of regions
$1,\dots,r-1$ and integrate over the (yet-to-play) strategies of regions
$r+1,\dots,R$. This is the regional analogue of Igami's cross-type
sequencing: it makes every sub-stage a single-agent problem whose CCP is a
\emph{scalar} fixed point rather than a joint fixed point across regions,
and it removes any coupling in the solver between regions at the same
stage.\\

Concretely, we solve each stage in reverse region order. Region~$R$'s scalar
FP is computed first for every reachable sub-state, and cached. Region~$R-1$'s
FP is then computed given the cached region-$R$ continuation, and so on
down to region~$1$. Across stages, the usual layered caches still apply:
stage-4 (PE) depends only on the post-new-stage state and is reused across
initial states via $\mathrm{EV}_{\mathrm{after\,pe}}[s_{an}, r]$; stages~3,
2, 1 cascade through $\mathrm{EV}_{\mathrm{after\,new}}$,
$\mathrm{EV}_{\mathrm{after\,both}}$, and $\mathrm{EV}_{\mathrm{after\,old}}$,
each keyed by $(\text{State}, r)$. Because each sub-state's CCP is a function
of the realized history, the per-region CCPs reported in the results below
are \emph{marginalised} over the equilibrium play of earlier-moving regions,
computed by a forward traversal of the sub-stage tree.\\

Because the state space now has $4R = 12$ integer coordinates, we cap
$N_{\max} = 6$ on this branch (about $1.8\times 10^4$ enumerated states) and
pick initial states with total firm count $\le 6$. One visible artefact of
the sequential-move convention is a small first-mover asymmetry: at any
symmetric $s_0$ with uniform $\gamma_r$, the three regions' CCPs differ by
at most $10^{-3}$. Region~1 commits without observing anyone, while
region~3 best-responds to the realised history. The gap is well below the
magnitude of every counterfactual difference reported in
Section~\ref{sec:merger}.

\section{Estimation on Simulated Data}
\label{sec:estimation}

As a first pass at recovering the model's structural parameters from data,
we simulate a firm-level dataset from the regional model under the
calibration of Table~\ref{tab:params} and then estimate
$(\gamma, \kappa, \phi)$, treating the demand parameters, the EVT1 scale
$\sigma$, the cost $c_{n,0}$, and the state cap $N_{\max}$ as known.

\subsection{Simulated dataset}

The simulator (\texttt{code/scripts/simulate\_data.jl}) draws \NumMarkets\
independent markets from the 2-period regional model. Each market gets its
own initial state $s_0 \sim \mathrm{unif}\{0,1,2\}^{4R}$ (rejecting empty or
over-capacity draws), so the panel covers a range of local cluster sizes.
For every firm in every period we record region, firm type, the two Cournot
quantities, and realised profit. For period~1 we additionally record the
discrete action. The structural parameters used to draw the data are
deliberately \emph{not} stored in the CSV.

\subsection{Two-step estimator}

Because equilibrium quantities are a deterministic function of the state
and the spillover vector $\gamma$, identification splits cleanly: $\gamma$
is pinned by the Cournot block and $(\kappa, \phi)$ by the discrete
actions, conditional on $\gamma$. We exploit this with a two-step estimator
(see \texttt{notes/estimation.md} for the full writeup).

\paragraph{Step 1: NLS for $\gamma$.}
For each market we reconstruct $s_0$ and $s_1$ by counting firms of each
type and region in the corresponding period. We then solve
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\gamma} \;=\; \arg\min_{\gamma}\;
  \sum_{m,t,r,\,\mathrm{slot}}
  \bigl( q^{\mathrm{obs}}_{m,t,r,\mathrm{slot}}
       - \hat{q}_{m,t,r,\mathrm{slot}}(\gamma) \bigr)^2,
  \label{eq:nls_gamma}
\end{equation}
starting from $\gamma^0 = (0,0,0)$ and leaving $\gamma$
\emph{unconstrained}. Because the simulated quantities are noiseless, the
minimum is machine zero ($\text{SSR} = \NlsSsr$) and $\widehat{\gamma}$
equals the truth to all reported digits; this step doubles as a sanity
check that state reconstruction is correct.

\paragraph{Step 2: MLE for $(\kappa, \phi)$.}
Holding $\gamma = \widehat{\gamma}$ fixed, we maximise the period-1 action
log-likelihood over $(\kappa, \phi)$. The likelihood \emph{replays} the
sequential-move order used by the solver: within each stage, regions act
in order $r = 1 \to 2 \to 3$, and each firm's contribution uses the stage
CCP evaluated at the sub-state its firm actually faced (not the marginal
CCP integrated over the forward traversal). Per market,
\begin{align}
  \ell_m(\kappa, \phi)
    &= \sum_{r=1}^{R}\Bigl[
         k^{\mathrm{so}}_r \log p_{\mathrm{so}}(\tilde{s}^{\mathrm{OLD}}_r)
       + k^{\mathrm{io}}_r \log p_{\mathrm{io}}(\tilde{s}^{\mathrm{OLD}}_r)
       + k^{\mathrm{eo}}_r \log\bigl(1 - p_{\mathrm{so}} - p_{\mathrm{io}}\bigr)
       \Bigr] \notag \\
    &\quad + \sum_{r=1}^{R}\Bigl[
         k^{\mathrm{sb}}_r \log p_{\mathrm{sb}}(\tilde{s}^{\mathrm{BOTH}}_r)
       + k^{\mathrm{eb}}_r \log\bigl(1 - p_{\mathrm{sb}}\bigr)
       \Bigr] \notag \\
    &\quad + \sum_{r=1}^{R}\Bigl[
         k^{\mathrm{sn}}_r \log p_{\mathrm{sn}}(\tilde{s}^{\mathrm{NEW}}_r)
       + k^{\mathrm{en}}_r \log\bigl(1 - p_{\mathrm{sn}}\bigr)
       \Bigr] \notag \\
    &\quad + \sum_{r=1}^{R}\Bigl[
         k^{\mathrm{ep}}_r \log p_{\mathrm{ep}}(\tilde{s}^{\mathrm{PE}}_r)
       + k^{\mathrm{op}}_r \log\bigl(1 - p_{\mathrm{ep}}\bigr)
       \Bigr],
  \label{eq:loglik_m}
\end{align}
where the $k^{\bullet}_r$ are observed action counts in region $r$ and the
$\tilde{s}^{\bullet}_r$ are the sub-states produced by sequentially
updating $s_0$ with the realised outcomes of earlier regions and stages.
The total objective is $\ell(\kappa, \phi) = \sum_m \ell_m(\kappa, \phi)$,
maximised by Nelder-Mead starting from $(\kappa^0, \phi^0) = (1.0, 1.0)$.
The starting point is intentionally far from the calibration, so the
optimiser must traverse the parameter space. Standard errors come from the
BHHH estimator $V = (\sum_m g_m g_m^\prime)^{-1}$ with central
finite-difference gradients.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:estimation} reports the estimates against the
data-generating parameters. The two-step estimator recovers
$\widehat{\gamma} = (\SpilloverOneHat, \SpilloverTwoHat, \SpilloverThreeHat)$

\subsection{Strawman: a misspecified model without agglomeration}
\label{sec:misspec}

What if an analyst sees the same Cournot data but never considers
agglomeration? They attribute cross-region cost differences to
exogenous regional fundamentals, factor prices and geography, and
estimate a region intercept on new-tech marginal cost while imposing
$\gamma \equiv 0$. The misspecified cost function is
\[
   c_{n,r}^{\text{mis}} = c_{n0} + \delta_r,
\]
state-independent within a region. Step~1 NLS on the same Cournot
data pins $\widehat{\delta}_r$ near
$-\widehat{\gamma}_r \cdot \mathbb{E}[n_b^r + n_n^r]$ and leaves a
residual SSR of $\MisspecNlsSsr$, against essentially zero for the
correctly-specified Step~1. Step~2 MLE on period-1 actions then
yields $\widetilde{\kappa} = \MisspecKappa$ and $\widetilde{\phi} =
\MisspecPhi$, both within a few percentage points of $\widehat{\kappa}$
and $\widehat{\phi}$.\\

The misspecified model fits action margins by construction but
mispredicts heterogeneity and comparative statics.
Table~\ref{tab:misspec} compares the two models on three blocks of
moments evaluated at the period-0 states observed in the data. The
innovation-rate panel is essentially a tie: both models match the
average $P(\text{innov} \mid \text{old})$ and
$P(\text{enter} \mid \text{pe})$ to four decimals, so the
innovator's-dilemma gap is identical. This is a feature of MLE.
Action frequencies are pinned at the realized states. The Lerner
panel and the comparative-static panel are where the strawman breaks
down.\\

\noindent \textit{Markup heterogeneity.}
On the new-generation side the misspecified model under-predicts both
the level and the dispersion of markups: mean Lerner index
$\MisspecLernerNew$ versus $\TrueLernerNew$, standard deviation
$\MisspecLernerNewSd$ versus $\TrueLernerNewSd$. By assumption the
misspecified marginal cost does not move with peer density, so
realized markups do not either. The true model captures the
cross-state covariance between cost and quantity that the
misspecified one collapses.\\

\noindent \textit{Comparative-static prediction.}
A counterfactual $+1$ \texttt{both}-firm shock to region~1 produces
$\Delta P(\text{innov} \mid \text{old}, r=1) = \MisspecDpioROne$ in
the misspecified model and $\TrueDpioROne$ in the true model
(firm-weighted across the period-0 states where the perturbation is
feasible). The drop is roughly three times larger under
misspecification. A misspecified analyst would conclude that peer
entry sharply discourages further innovation. The true story is
much milder, because the cost-reduction channel partially offsets
the negative price effect of an additional new-generation supplier.
The gap between the two values is a one-number summary of the
agglomeration externality the strawman is missing.\\

\section{Counterfactual: Region-3 Innovation Subsidy}
\label{sec:subsidy}

A region-3 innovation subsidy of size $\tau$ shifts the \emph{private}
cost of innovation in region~3 from $\widehat{\kappa}$ to
$(\widehat{\kappa} - \tau)$ while leaving the social resource cost
unchanged at $\widehat{\kappa}$. Sweeping $\tau$ over the grid
$\tau / \widehat{\kappa} \in \{0, 0.10, \ldots, 0.50\}$ produces a
clean planner-vs-country split: region~3's own welfare is weakly
\emph{increasing} in $\tau$, but aggregate welfare across the three
regions has a small interior optimum at
$\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyOptimumFrac$
($\Delta\Sigma W = \SubsidyOptimumDWPct\,\%$). At the largest grid
point $\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyMaxFrac$, region~3's
$P(\mathrm{innov} \mid \mathrm{old})$ rises by
$\SubsidyMaxInnovPctRThree\,\%$ and its welfare gains
$\SubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$, but regions~1 and~2 lose
$|\SubsidyMaxDWPctROne|\,\%$ and $|\SubsidyMaxDWPctRTwo|\,\%$, so the
planner stops well short of the corner. The wedge between region~3's
preferred subsidy and the planner's is the cross-region Cournot
externality: the subsidy makes region~3's new-tech producers cheaper
in a globally competitive product market, and the producers in
regions~1 and~2 lose share. The rest of the section sets up the
subsidy specification, derives the welfare formula under sovereign
funding, describes the Monte Carlo evaluation, and walks through the
mechanism.

\subsection{Subsidy specification}

A region-$r$ innovation subsidy of size $\tau_r \ge 0$ changes only the
\emph{private} payoff of an old-tech firm switching to ``both''
technology:
\begin{equation}
  u^{\mathrm{innov}}_r
    = \pi^{\mathrm{both}}_r(s) - (\kappa - \tau_r)
      + \beta\, \mathrm{EV}^{\mathrm{old}\to\mathrm{both}}_r,
  \label{eq:subsidy_payoff}
\end{equation}
leaving the entry decision and the rest of the game unchanged. The
implementation adds a single \texttt{subsidy} field to the parameter
struct; the default \texttt{(0,0,0)} recovers the
no-subsidy baseline. We sweep the one-region policy
$\tau = (0, 0, \tau)$ to isolate the question ``what does targeted
industrial policy in region~3 do?'' from the broader question of a
global R\&D subsidy. The spillover specification stays at the local-only
baseline of equation~\eqref{eq:local_agglom}.

\subsection{Welfare under sovereign funding}

Each region is treated as a sovereign country: its own government funds
its own subsidy from its own taxpayers, so the transfer
$\tau_r \cdot k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r$ flows from region~$r$'s taxpayers to
region~$r$'s firms and cancels exactly inside region~$r$'s welfare. The
\emph{social} resource cost remains the full $\kappa$ even when the firm
only pays $(\kappa - \tau_r)$; the subsidy reduces the private burden,
not the real labour and materials embodied in a new-technology
blueprint. Two-period discounted welfare per region is therefore
\begin{equation}
  W_r
    = \tfrac{1}{R}\bigl[\mathrm{CS}(s_0) + \beta\, \mathrm{CS}(s_1)\bigr]
    + \mathrm{PS}_r(s_0) + \beta\, \mathrm{PS}_r(s_1)
    - \kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r - \phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r,
  \label{eq:welfare_subsidy}
\end{equation}
identical in form to equation~\eqref{eq:welfare}: the subsidy is a
within-region transfer that does not appear in $W_r$. With this
accounting, the identity
$\sum_r W_r = \mathrm{CS}_{\mathrm{total}} + \sum_r \mathrm{PS}_r
- \sum_r (\kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r + \phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r)$
holds exactly in the baseline and in every grid scenario. The runner
verifies it numerically and finds residuals at machine precision.

\subsection{Evaluation by Monte Carlo with common random numbers}

We average $K = \SubsidyNMarkets$ independent simulations per scenario.

contributes paired observations $(W_r^{\mathrm{base}},
W_r^{\tau})$, so the variance of the welfare difference is much smaller
than the variance of either level. Headline numbers stabilise to roughly
$0.05$ percentage points by $K = 1000$.

\subsection{Discussion}

Three points are worth highlighting.\\

\textbf{The subsidy works on its target margin.} Region~3's
$P(\mathrm{innov} \mid \mathrm{old})$ rises from $\SubsidyBaseInnovRThree$
to $\SubsidyMaxInnovRThree$ at $\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyMaxFrac$,
a $\SubsidyMaxInnovPctRThree\,\%$ lift. The mechanism is direct: a lower
private cost moves the threshold draw of the EVT1 shock that triggers
innovation. As a policy lever for raising regional R\&D activity, the
subsidy works.\\

\textbf{Region~3 alone wants the subsidy maxed; the planner does not.}
A subsidy is welfare-improving for the planner only if there is a
positive externality on the subsidised margin that the firm fails to
internalise. In our calibration the agglomeration externality
$\widehat{\gamma}_r = \SubsidyGammaHat$ is \emph{local}: region~3's
innovation today helps only region~3's own period-2 cost, and the firm
already internalises the bulk of that benefit through its expected
period-2 profit. Cournot competition across regions, in contrast, makes
region-3 expansion a \emph{negative} externality on regions~1 and~2,
which the planner does internalise. Region~3 alone sees only the gain
and prefers $\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyMaxFrac$; the planner
balances the gain against the cross-region loss and settles on the
interior $\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyOptimumFrac$.\\

\textbf{The non-treated regions bear small but consistent losses.}
Cheaper region-3 production drags the global new-technology price down,
so regions~1 and~2 see lower firm profits. The CS share they receive
from cheaper goods is too small to compensate. At
$\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyMaxFrac$, region~1 loses
$|\SubsidyMaxDWPctROne|\,\%$ of its baseline welfare and region~2 loses
$|\SubsidyMaxDWPctRTwo|\,\%$. These are small but signed in the same
direction across all $K \in \{500, 1000, 5000\}$.\\

The result is calibration-dependent. Under a spillover specification
that pools region~3 with another region (an alliance variant of
Appendix~\ref{sec:merger}), the planner would internalise the spillover
from region-3 innovation onto its partner and the optimum would shift
right. With a larger $\widehat{\gamma}$ or a tighter $\rho$, the
cross-region effects scale accordingly. Full details, including the
K-stability diagnostic and the sovereign-funding accounting, are in
\texttt{notes/subsidy\_counterfactual.md}.

\section{Counterfactual: Region-3 Entry Subsidy}
\label{sec:entry_subsidy}

A region-3 \emph{entry} subsidy of size $\psi$ shifts the private cost
of entering as a new-technology firm in region~3 from $\widehat{\phi}$
to $(\widehat{\phi} - \psi)$ while leaving the social resource cost of
entry unchanged at $\widehat{\phi}$. The same planner-vs-country split
appears as in the innovation case, but at a different optimum: region~3
alone prefers $\psi / \widehat{\phi} = \EntrySubsidyMaxFrac$ (its
welfare rises by $\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ at the corner),
while the planner picks the interior $\psi / \widehat{\phi} =
\EntrySubsidyOptimumFrac$ ($\Delta\Sigma W =
\EntrySubsidyOptimumDWPct\,\%$). At the corner, regions~1 and~2 lose
$|\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctROne|\,\%$ and $|\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctRTwo|\,\%$
respectively, so the cross-region loss eats up the own-region gain.\\

The entry-subsidy story differs from the innovation-subsidy story in
two structural ways, both visible in the numbers. First, region~3's
own welfare gain at $\psi/\widehat{\phi} = 0.5$ is
$\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ versus $\SubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$
under the innovation subsidy --- because new entrants add new-tech
quantity without cannibalising existing old-gen revenue, the
own-region pass-through is larger per dollar of subsidy. Second, the
larger pass-through also feeds back into larger cross-region losses,
so the planner's optimal entry subsidy ($\EntrySubsidyOptimumFrac$) is
\emph{smaller} than its optimal innovation subsidy
($\SubsidyOptimumFrac$): the cleaner instrument also has a steeper
externality cost, and the planner stops earlier on the entry axis.

\subsection{Subsidy specification}

A region-$r$ entry subsidy of size $\psi_r \ge 0$ changes only the
\emph{private} payoff of a potential entrant becoming a new-tech firm:
\begin{equation}
  u^{\mathrm{enter}}_r = -(\phi - \psi_r) + \beta\,
  \mathrm{EV}^{\mathrm{pe}\to\mathrm{new}}_r,
  \label{eq:entry_subsidy_payoff}
\end{equation}
leaving the innovation decision and the rest of the game unchanged.
The implementation adds a single \texttt{entry\_subsidy} field to the
parameter struct; the default \texttt{(0,0,0)} recovers the
no-subsidy baseline. We sweep the one-region policy
$\psi = (0, 0, \psi)$ to isolate targeted entry policy in region~3
from the question of a global entry subsidy. The spillover
specification stays at the local-only baseline.

\subsection{Welfare under sovereign funding}

As in the innovation-subsidy section, region~$r$'s government funds
its own subsidy from its own taxpayers, so the transfer
$\psi_r \cdot k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r$ flows from region~$r$'s taxpayers
to region~$r$'s entrants and cancels exactly inside region~$r$'s
welfare. The social resource cost of an entry remains the full
$\phi$. Two-period discounted welfare per region is
\begin{equation}
  W_r
    = \tfrac{1}{R}\bigl[\mathrm{CS}(s_0) + \beta\, \mathrm{CS}(s_1)\bigr]
    + \mathrm{PS}_r(s_0) + \beta\, \mathrm{PS}_r(s_1)
    - \kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r - \phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r,
  \label{eq:welfare_entry_subsidy}
\end{equation}
identical in form to equation~\eqref{eq:welfare}: the entry subsidy is
a within-region transfer that does not appear in $W_r$. The identity
$\sum_r W_r = \mathrm{CS}_{\mathrm{total}} + \sum_r \mathrm{PS}_r
- \sum_r (\kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r + \phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r)$
holds at machine precision in baseline and in every grid scenario.

\subsection{Evaluation by Monte Carlo with common random numbers}

We average $K = \EntrySubsidyNMarkets$ independent simulations per
scenario, with the same seed (\EntrySubsidySeed) across baseline and
every grid point and per-market \texttt{MersenneTwister(seed + $k$)}.
Headline numbers stabilise to roughly $0.05$ percentage points by
$K = 1000$; the aggregate welfare effect is small (about a basis
point) but its sign is robust across $K$.

\subsection{Discussion}

Three points are worth highlighting.\\

\textbf{Entry pulls a cleaner margin than innovation.} Innovating
firms are old-tech incumbents that switch to ``both''. They were
already producing old-tech output, and the new-tech output they add
as ``both'' partly cannibalises that old-tech revenue through
cross-market substitution $\rho$. New entrants, in contrast, arrive
with no legacy old-tech output to displace. Each marginal entry
therefore adds more total new-tech quantity at the global Cournot
stage than each marginal innovation does. With $\kappa = \phi$ in our
calibration, the per-dollar private-cost reduction is the same under
the two policies, so the difference shows up directly in region~3's
welfare gain: at the corner, $\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ under
entry versus $\SubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ under innovation. The
cross-region losses scale up correspondingly:
$|\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctROne|\,\%$ for region~1 versus
$|\SubsidyMaxDWPctROne|\,\%$ under innovation.\\

\textbf{The planner wants both subsidies, but at modest interior
levels.} Under the innovation subsidy alone the planner's optimum is
$\tau / \widehat{\kappa} = \SubsidyOptimumFrac$ ($\Delta\Sigma W =
\SubsidyOptimumDWPct\,\%$); under the entry subsidy alone it is
$\psi / \widehat{\phi} = \EntrySubsidyOptimumFrac$ ($\Delta\Sigma W =
\EntrySubsidyOptimumDWPct\,\%$). The entry-subsidy optimum is smaller
in absolute terms because the entry margin has higher pass-through and
therefore a steeper marginal externality cost on regions~1 and~2: the
planner accepts the cleaner instrument at a smaller dose. In both
cases, region~3 alone would prefer the corner --- the wedge between
its preferred subsidy and the planner's is exactly the cross-region
externality that the planner internalises and region~3 does not.\\

\textbf{The aggregate effects are small but signed.} Both interior
optima deliver a $\Delta\Sigma W$ on the order of $0.01\,\%$ of
baseline. This is small in level, but the interior optimum is what
makes the planner-vs-region-3 split well-defined: region~3 has a
strictly preferred policy (the corner) and the planner has a strictly
different one (the interior). The joint sweep below sharpens this
contrast on the two-instrument grid.\\

\subsection{Joint subsidy grid}
\label{sec:joint_subsidy}

A joint sweep over $(\tau, \psi)$ on the $\JointSubsidyGridLengthTau
\times \JointSubsidyGridLengthPsi$ grid lets the planner trade the two
instruments off against each other. We use it to make three points,
which the heatmap of Figure~\ref{fig:joint_subsidy_heatmap}
summarises. The two markers on the heatmap correspond to the
planner's argmax under the two calibrations: the gold star is the
agglomeration calibration $\gamma = \widehat{\gamma}$; the white
diamond is the no-agglomeration counterfactual $\gamma = 0$.

\paragraph{(1) The planner's optimal subsidy is asymmetric: more entry
than innovation.} At the agglomeration calibration the planner picks
$(\tau/\widehat{\kappa}, \psi/\widehat{\phi}) =
(\JointSubsidyOptTauFrac, \JointSubsidyOptPsiFrac)$, with the
entry-subsidy fraction substantially larger than the innovation-subsidy
fraction. The mechanism is the cannibalization wedge of Igami's
``innovator's dilemma'': an old-tech incumbent who switches to
``both'' adds new-tech output but partly cannibalises its own old-tech
revenue, so it produces \emph{less} new-tech quantity than a fresh
entrant of equivalent type. A unit of entry subsidy therefore moves
the new-tech market further than a unit of innovation subsidy. The
planner internalises this and tilts the mix toward entry; the
own-region welfare-per-corner numbers in
Sections~\ref{sec:subsidy} and~\ref{sec:entry_subsidy} confirm this
($\EntrySubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ for entry vs.\
$\SubsidyMaxDWPctRThree\,\%$ for innovation at $\psi/\widehat{\phi} =
\tau/\widehat{\kappa} = 0.5$).

\paragraph{(2) Agglomeration is the externality the planner is
correcting.} Under $\gamma = \widehat{\gamma} = \SubsidyGammaHat$, the
local agglomeration spillover is a positive externality: adding an
innovator (or entrant, since both end up using new tech) lowers next
period's $c_{n,r}$ for every other new-tech firm in region~$r$. The
firm internalises only its own period-2 cost reduction; the rest of
the cluster gets it for free. A subsidy that nudges $P(\mathrm{innov})$
or $P(\mathrm{enter})$ above the laissez-faire level helps close the
gap. With $\gamma$ shut off, that channel disappears and only the
\emph{negative} cross-region Cournot externality remains. The planner
then prefers \emph{no} subsidy at all: the no-agglomeration argmax is
the corner $(\tau/\widehat{\kappa}, \psi/\widehat{\phi}) =
(\JointSubsidyNoAgglomOptTauFrac, \JointSubsidyNoAgglomOptPsiFrac)$,
with $\Delta\Sigma W = \JointSubsidyNoAgglomOptDWPct\,\%$, and every
positive cell in the no-agglomeration grid is welfare-decreasing
(largest cell $\JointSubsidyNoAgglomBothMaxDWPct\,\%$). The size of
the optimal subsidy is therefore proportional to the strength of the
agglomeration externality, not a robust feature of the model.

\paragraph{(3) A unilateral region-3 government over-subsidises.}
Region~3's own welfare is monotone non-decreasing across the entire
$(\tau, \psi)$ grid: it picks the corner
$(\JointSubsidyROptTauFrac, \JointSubsidyROptPsiFrac)$, where
$\Delta W_3 = \JointSubsidyROptDWPctRThree\,\%$. The planner stops far
short of the corner because the planner internalises the
producer-surplus losses imposed on regions~1 and~2 by cheaper
region-3 output and region~3 does not. The wedge between region~3's
preferred policy and the planner's is exactly the cross-region
Cournot terms-of-trade externality. It would shrink if the spillover
specification pooled region~3 with another region, since the planner
would then internalise more of the spillover gain to region~3's
partner (cf.\ the alliance variant of Appendix~\ref{sec:merger}).
Full details, including the K-stability diagnostic, are in
\texttt{notes/entry\_subsidy\_counterfactual.md}.

\section{Next Steps}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Heterogeneous absorptive capacity.} The alliance result
        here uses uniform $\widehat{\gamma}_r$. Allowing $\gamma_r$ to
        differ across regions, with the bloc-pooled spec of
        equation~\eqref{eq:bloc_pool}, would let one region's higher
        absorptive capacity amplify the alliance's effect.
  \item \textbf{Fiscal cost of policy.} The current welfare accounting
        prices in firms' own costs paid but does not net out the fiscal
        cost of any subsidy a government would need to deliver to enlarge
        $\gamma_r$ in a target region. Adding government revenue and cost
        makes the targeted-subsidy and alliance scenarios directly
        comparable as policy levers.
  \item \textbf{$T > 2$ horizon.} A genuine multi-period extension would
        force the value-function recursion to use the
        $\sigma \log \sum \exp$ ex-ante form at every internal period.
        The current 2-period setup avoids this because period~2 is
        terminal.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Counterfactual: EU--US Alliance}
\label{sec:merger}

Pooling regions~1 and~2's innovator counts lifts welfare by roughly
$\MergerWelfPctROne\,\%$ in each allied region, leaves region~3 unchanged
in welfare terms, and raises aggregate welfare by $\MergerWelfPctTotal\,\%$
of baseline. Innovation rates rise by $\MergerInnovPctROne\,\%$ and
$\MergerInnovPctRTwo\,\%$ in the allied regions and fall by
$|\MergerInnovPctRThree|\,\%$ in region~3, so the unallied region is harmed
on the real-economy margin even though it breaks even on welfare. The rest
of the section sets up the spillover specification, derives the welfare
formula, describes the Monte Carlo evaluation, and walks through the
mechanism.

\subsection{Spillover specification under the alliance}

Under the baseline, region~$r$'s new-technology marginal cost depends only
on its own innovators, equation~\eqref{eq:local_agglom}. Under the alliance
of $\{1, 2\}$, regions~1 and~2 share an enlarged pool:
\begin{equation}
  c_{n,r,t}^{\mathrm{alliance}}
    = \max\!\left\{0,\; c_{n,0} - \gamma_r \cdot \!\sum_{r' \in \mathrm{bloc}(r)}\!
      \bigl(n_{b,r',t} + n_{n,r',t}\bigr)\right\},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{bloc}(1) = \mathrm{bloc}(2) = \{1, 2\},\
  \mathrm{bloc}(3) = \{3\}.
  \label{eq:bloc_pool}
\end{equation}
The cost floor prevents the marginal cost from turning negative; under the
calibration $\gamma \le 0.15$ this constraint never binds. The implementation
adds a single \texttt{blocs} field to the parameter struct; the default
\texttt{(1,2,3)} recovers equation~\eqref{eq:local_agglom} bit-for-bit.

\subsection{Welfare}

The two-market linear demand of
equations~\eqref{eq:demand_o}--\eqref{eq:demand_n} is rationalised by the
quasi-linear utility $U = A(Q_o + Q_n) - \tfrac{b}{2}(Q_o^2 + Q_n^2) - s\,
Q_o\, Q_n$ with $b = B/M$ and $s = \rho B/M$, giving consumer surplus
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CS}(s) = \tfrac{b}{2}(Q_o^2 + Q_n^2) + s\, Q_o\, Q_n.
  \label{eq:cs}
\end{equation}
Because Cournot is global, $\mathrm{CS}$ is a single number; we allocate
it equally across regions (equal-population assumption). Producer surplus
is local: each firm's per-period profit is charged to its own region.
Innovation and entry costs are paid by the firm's region in period~1.
Two-period discounted welfare per region is
\begin{equation}
  W_r
    = \tfrac{1}{R}\bigl[\mathrm{CS}(s_0) + \beta\,\mathrm{CS}(s_1)\bigr]
    + \mathrm{PS}_r(s_0) + \beta\,\mathrm{PS}_r(s_1)
    - \kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r - \phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r,
  \label{eq:welfare}
\end{equation}
which satisfies $\sum_r W_r = \mathrm{CS}_{\mathrm{total}} +
\sum_r \mathrm{PS}_r - \sum_r (\kappa\, k^{\mathrm{innov}}_r +
\phi\, k^{\mathrm{enter}}_r)$ by construction. The runner verifies this
identity numerically and finds residuals at machine precision.

\subsection{Discussion}

Three points are worth highlighting.\\

\textbf{Allied regions gain in two ways.} Their pooled innovator count
lowers $c_{n,1}$ and $c_{n,2}$ whenever \emph{either} region has an
innovator, raising the period-2 profit of being on new tech. This pulls
more firms into innovation and entry there, which raises producer surplus,
and the additional new-tech output also lowers prices globally, raising
the regions' share of consumer surplus. Welfare rises by
$\MergerWelfPctROne\,\%$ in region~1 and $\MergerWelfPctRTwo\,\%$ in
region~2.\\

\textbf{Region~3 loses on innovation but breaks even on welfare.} Tougher
global Cournot competition from cheaper allied competitors drags region~3's
$P(\mathrm{innov})$ and $P(\mathrm{enter})$ down by about $4\,\%$ and
$3\,\%$ respectively, and its $\mathrm{PS}_3$ falls by roughly $7\,\%$.
Its share of the global CS gain is almost the same magnitude in the
opposite direction, so its net welfare moves by less than a basis point
($\MergerWelfPctRThree\,\%$). The unallied region is harmed in real-economy
terms (less innovation, lower profits) but the consumer-side benefit of
cheaper goods produced by the alliance compensates almost exactly.\\

\textbf{The aggregate gain is positive and sizeable.} Total welfare rises
by $\MergerWelfPctTotal\,\%$ of baseline. Roughly half of the gain accrues
to each allied region, with a near-zero residual from region~3. Knowledge
integration is a positive-sum game even before factoring in the aggregate
consumer benefits.\\

The result depends on the calibration. Under an earlier
$(\gamma, \sigma) = (0.05, 1.0)$ setting the same alliance moved welfare by
under $1\,\%$ everywhere; tripling $\gamma$ scales the spillover delivered
by the merger, and halving $\sigma$ doubles the logit slope $P(1-P)/\sigma$
against any payoff change, so the combined effect on equilibrium quantities
is roughly $6\times$. The recalibrated values stay well below the cost-floor
saturation that sets in at $\gamma \gtrsim 0.30$, so the model is in its
responsive interior. Full details, including the K-stability diagnostic and
the $\sigma\!\cdot\!\gamma_{\mathrm{em}}$ welfare convention, are in
\texttt{notes/merger\_counterfactual.md}.
